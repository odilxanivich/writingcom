Many people consider shopping malls as great places to spend their leisure time and meet others. People in the past, however, visited shopping malls only when necessary. To what extent do you think this is a negative trend?

It is often argued that shopping malls have transformed from mere commercial centres into popular leisure destinations. While this development may bring some social benefits, I believe the trend is largely negative for both individuals and society.

On the positive side, shopping malls provide safe and accessible environments for families and young people. They often include entertainment facilities such as cinemas, restaurants, and play areas, which allow people to socialize and spend quality time together. In many urban areas, malls also serve as indoor recreational spaces that are convenient and secure compared to public parks or streets.

Nevertheless, the drawbacks of this phenomenon outweigh the benefits. First, associating leisure primarily with consumption reinforces materialism and consumer culture. People, especially the younger generation, may begin to equate happiness with shopping, which can lead to financial stress or unhealthy spending habits. Second, reliance on malls reduces engagement in healthier activities such as sports, outdoor recreation, or community events, contributing indirectly to sedentary lifestyles. Finally, this trend undermines local businesses and public spaces, as small retailers and traditional markets struggle to compete with large commercial complexes.

In conclusion, while shopping malls can offer entertainment, their role as the dominant leisure option is problematic. Encouraging more balanced recreational activities outside the sphere of consumerism would bring greater long-term benefits to individuals and society.
